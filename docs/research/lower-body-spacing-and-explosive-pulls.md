# Restructuring lower body spacing and explosive pulls for functional athletes

**The two biggest fixes for this program are straightforward: space heavy lower body days 4+ days apart (not 1), and swap the snatch-grip high pull for dumbbell high pulls.** Research consistently shows 48–72 hours minimum recovery between heavy lower body sessions targeting similar muscle groups, meaning the current Sunday-to-Tuesday gap virtually guarantees underperformance. For the explosive pull, dumbbell hang high pulls deliver equal or greater peak power than barbell Olympic variations while eliminating the coordination and grip issues entirely. Below is the evidence and practical programming for both changes.

## Why one day between heavy leg sessions fails

The current Sunday heavy squats → Tuesday lower body structure leaves only **48 hours** between sessions, but the real problem is worse: Tuesday's hip thrusts, Bulgarian split squats, and RDLs hammer much of the same musculature that's still recovering from Sunday's back squats. Belcher et al. (2019) tested well-trained males performing 4 sets to failure at 80% 1RM on back squats and found that muscle damage markers (creatine kinase, lactate dehydrogenase) and average concentric velocity required **up to 72 hours** to return to baseline. A separate study on squat reproducibility found a **-12.6% performance decrement** when retested at just 24 hours. A 2024 review in *PMC* confirmed the consensus: "Greater recovery times are needed for the lower body (48–72 hours) compared to 24 hours or less for the upper body."

One critical nuance changes the calculus, though. González-Badillo et al. (2016) demonstrated that **avoiding training to failure** cuts recovery time dramatically — protocols stopped well short of failure recovered in 24–48 hours versus 72+ hours for sets taken to failure. Additionally, the popular notion of multi-day "CNS fatigue" from heavy squats is largely a myth. Barnes et al. (2019) had trained men perform 8×2 at 95% 1RM for squats and deadlifts and found only a 5–10% reduction in central neural output, with no difference between exercises. Howatson et al. (2016) studied elite athletes squatting 190+ kg and found **zero central fatigue** — all fatigue was peripheral. What people attribute to "CNS burnout" is really muscle damage, metabolic stress, and DOMS.

This distinction matters because it means varying movement patterns (squat-dominant versus hip-hinge-dominant) and load characteristics (heavy versus explosive) allows closer spacing than repeating identical sessions. The fatigue is in the tissue, not the nervous system.

## How proven periodization models space lower body work

The **Conjugate/Westside method** places max effort lower body on Monday and dynamic effort lower body on Friday — roughly **4 days apart**. The max effort session generates high muscular fatigue from near-maximal loads, while the dynamic effort session uses sub-maximal loads (50–60% bar weight plus bands/chains) moved at maximal speed, producing far less peripheral damage. This mirrors exactly what the athlete needs: one heavy day and one power/speed day with different fatigue profiles.

**Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)**, researched by Dr. Mike Zourdos and popularized by Greg Nuckols, takes the opposite approach — training the same movement pattern 3× per week by alternating heavy (5×3 at 85%), moderate/hypertrophy (3×10 at 70%), and power (6×2 at 75%) sessions. Rhea et al. (2002) found DUP produced roughly **2× the strength improvements** versus linear periodization over 12 weeks. The key insight is that not every session creates maximum fatigue, so the same muscles can tolerate 48-hour spacing when intensity undulates.

Standard **Push/Pull/Legs** splits separate leg days by about 3 days (Monday and Thursday in a PPL-PPL rotation), often differentiating quad-dominant and hamstring-dominant sessions. **Upper/Lower** splits achieve similar spacing. The athlete's three planned lower body sessions — heavy squat, heavy hinge/unilateral, and athletic/power — essentially constitute a DUP-style undulation already, with each session occupying a different point on the force-velocity curve.

## The recommended weekly layout

Given 6 training days plus 1 active recovery day, the optimal configuration places heavy lower body sessions **4 days apart** with the athletic/power day serving as a neural bridge between them:

| Day | Session | Focus |
|-----|---------|-------|
| **Sunday** | Heavy squat-dominant lower | Back squats (top sets of 3–5), front squats, leg press, quad accessories |
| **Monday** | Upper body push | Bench press, OHP, triceps, chest |
| **Tuesday** | Upper body pull | Rows, pull-ups, biceps, rear delts |
| **Wednesday** | Athletic/power lower | Plyometrics, sled work, agility drills, box jumps, sprint work |
| **Thursday** | Heavy hip-hinge/unilateral lower | RDLs, Bulgarian split squats, hip thrusts, hamstring curls |
| **Friday** | Power upper + core | Explosive pulls, overhead work, arms, conditioning |
| **Saturday** | Active recovery | Mobility, foam rolling, light movement, ankle/foot work |

This layout delivers **4 full days** between heavy sessions (Sunday → Thursday), places recovery day directly before the hardest session, and positions the athletic day as a bridge on Wednesday. Two upper body days between heavy lower sessions prevent interference while maintaining upper body volume.

An alternative "maximum separation" layout pushes the heavy hinge day to Friday (5 days after Sunday squats), moves the athletic day to Tuesday, and places active recovery on Wednesday as a mid-week reset. This sacrifices some upper body training time but maximizes lower body recovery for athletes who find even 4 days insufficient.

## Why the athletic day belongs between heavy days

Research supports placing the power/agility session between the two heavy days for three reasons. First, plyometric and agility work taxes the **elastic/neural system** (stretch-shortening cycle, motor unit recruitment speed) while causing relatively less peripheral muscle damage than heavy eccentrically-loaded strength work. Second, moderate-intensity explosive work can actually **enhance subsequent performance** through medium-term post-activation potentiation — Joel Smith's research at Just Fly Sports supports the idea that moving from high-magnitude to low-magnitude exercises leaves athletes "feeling fresh and ready to express power." Third, sled work specifically is **concentric-only**, meaning it produces minimal DOMS and enhances recovery through increased blood flow. Louie Simmons built his entire restoration philosophy around sled drags between heavy sessions.

Keep the athletic day's plyometric volume moderate: **80–120 ground contacts** per session, with full recovery between sets (2–3 minutes for jumps, 1–2 for agility). Content should include box jumps (3–4 × 5), broad jumps (3 × 5), lateral bounds (3 × 6/side), agility ladder or cone work (10–15 minutes), and sled pushes or drags (6–8 × 20–30 meters). This session should feel neurally demanding but not muscularly crushing. One essential caveat: avoid high-volume plyometrics the day *after* heavy squats if the athlete experiences significant DOMS, as explosive movements on fatigued muscles increase injury risk.

## Dumbbell high pulls are the ideal snatch-grip replacement

The dumbbell hang high pull solves every issue the athlete experienced. It uses a **neutral grip** (eliminating the wide snatch grip that caused problems), requires minimal coordination (learning curve of roughly 2 out of 5), and needs only dumbbells. Critically, research by Takei et al. (2021) in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* found that hang high pulls produced **significantly greater peak power** than hang power cleans at 40%, 60%, and 70% of 1RM — because there's no catch phase forcing the athlete to decelerate the implement, every rep expresses maximum power.

The movement pattern is simple: from a hang position (dumbbells at mid-thigh), drive through the hips with triple extension and pull the dumbbells explosively to shoulder height, elbows high. It targets the traps, rear and side deltoids, and rhomboids while incorporating hip drive from the glutes and hamstrings — a perfect counterbalance to the pressing movements that follow. Coach Bob Alejo at SimpliFaster argues that the catch phase in cleans often *limits* power expression because athletes slow the bar to get under it rather than pulling maximally. Removing the catch is a feature, not a bug.

Program dumbbell high pulls **first in the session** before any pressing work. Per NSCA guidelines, the most technically and physically demanding exercise should be performed while the athlete is neurally fresh. Use **4 sets of 4 reps** with 2–3 minutes rest, focusing on maximal speed and intent. Load should be approximately 60–80% of what feels heavy — if the dumbbells can't move fast, they're too heavy for power work. A sensible 6-week progression: weeks 1–2 at 3×5 (technique focus), weeks 3–4 at 4×4 (moderate load, speed emphasis), weeks 5–6 at 5×3 (heavier, maximum intent), then deload at 3×3.

## Four other viable alternatives worth considering

If the athlete wants variety or progression beyond dumbbell high pulls, four alternatives rank well. The **single-arm dumbbell snatch** (rated 9/10 for power development) is the most athletic option, adding unilateral core challenge and overhead stability, though its moderate learning curve (3/5) and overhead catch position require adequate shoulder mobility. The **clean-grip hang high pull** with a barbell is the most direct substitute for the snatch-grip version — same explosive pattern, narrower grip — but requires controlled lowering without bumper plates. **Medicine ball overhead slams** have virtually zero learning curve and work as a neural primer, though they can't be meaningfully progressively loaded and function better as a supplementary tool than a primary power builder. Finally, the **landmine clean and press** uses equipment the athlete already has and combines explosive hip drive with upper body work, though its pressing component creates some overlap with the OHP and landmine press already programmed.

Cables and bands were evaluated but fall short: their increasing-tension resistance profile is the opposite of what power development requires, where the goal is to accelerate through the entire range of motion. Battle ropes and kettlebell swings are useful conditioning tools but don't provide the upper body explosive pulling stimulus needed to balance pressing work on this day.

## Conclusion

The path forward rests on two evidence-based changes. First, **move the heavy hinge day to Thursday** (or Friday) to create 4–5 days of separation from Sunday's heavy squats, with the athletic/power day on Wednesday serving as a low-damage neural bridge rather than an additional recovery burden. This alone should eliminate the fatigue carryover problem. Second, **replace the snatch-grip high pull with dumbbell hang high pulls** — they produce greater peak power than cleans according to research, require a fraction of the coordination, and create an ideal pull-to-push balance on power upper day. The broader principle unifying both fixes: match the training stimulus to the recovery capacity by varying movement patterns, intensity zones, and fatigue profiles across the week rather than stacking similar demands on consecutive days.
